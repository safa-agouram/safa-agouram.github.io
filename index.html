Mapping Dead Neurons: Opening the Black Box of a Breast-Cancer Screening Network

Deep networks used to read mammograms are, to their own designers, largely opaque. We built a generalist diagnostic tool that takes any PyTorch model and any dataloader and returns a map of which neurons never fire — then pointed it at GMIC, a published, radiologist-competitive breast-cancer screening model, to see what its blind spots look like.

01 — Introduction

AI is already reading mammograms. It rarely explains itself.

Screening mammography is read twice, by two radiologists, precisely because a missed cancer is costly and a single reader is fallible. Over the last decade, deep convolutional networks have entered this pipeline as a plausible third reader: models such as GMIC match or exceed radiologist-level performance on held-out screening exams, at a fraction of the inference cost of a generic ResNet. That result is genuinely useful. It is also, from the point of view of anyone who has to trust the model's output, almost entirely opaque.

Working definition

We call a neuron dead if, across an entire evaluation set, its activation never exceeds zero after a ReLU. A dead ReLU unit is mathematically inert: its gradient is zero, it contributes nothing to the forward pass, and it will never learn again.

02 — Why map dead neurons

A cartography of what the network stopped using

Interpretability research on medical imaging models has mostly focused on where a network is looking — saliency maps, attention overlays, Grad-CAM heatmaps drawn on top of the mammogram itself. That work matters, but there is a second, orthogonal question that saliency maps do not answer: not where the network looks, but which of its internal units are doing any work at all.
